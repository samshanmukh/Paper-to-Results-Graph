Title: Adam: A Method for Stochastic Optimization
Authors: Diederik P. Kingma, Jimmy Ba
Year: 2014
arXiv: 1412.6980

[Excerpt — abstract and key sections condensed for hackathon demo]

ABSTRACT
We introduce Adam, an algorithm for first-order gradient-based optimization of
stochastic objective functions, based on adaptive estimates of lower-order
moments. The method is straightforward to implement, is computationally
efficient, has little memory requirements, is invariant to diagonal rescaling
of the gradients, and is well suited for problems that are large in terms of
data and/or parameters. The hyper-parameters have intuitive interpretations
and typically require little tuning. Empirical results demonstrate that Adam
works well in practice and compares favorably to other stochastic optimization
methods.

METHOD
Adam maintains exponential moving averages of the gradient (m_t) and the
squared gradient (v_t), with decay rates beta1 and beta2. The moving averages
are bias-corrected, and parameters are updated as:

    m_t = beta1 * m_{t-1} + (1 - beta1) * g_t
    v_t = beta2 * v_{t-1} + (1 - beta2) * g_t^2
    m_hat = m_t / (1 - beta1^t)
    v_hat = v_t / (1 - beta2^t)
    theta_t = theta_{t-1} - alpha * m_hat / (sqrt(v_hat) + epsilon)

Default settings: alpha=0.001, beta1=0.9, beta2=0.999, epsilon=1e-8.

CLAIMS
1. Adam converges faster than SGD and other first-order methods on the tested
   problems (logistic regression on MNIST, multi-layer neural networks,
   convolutional networks).
2. Adam requires little hyperparameter tuning; the default settings work well
   across a broad range of problems.
3. The magnitude of parameter updates is invariant to diagonal rescaling of
   the gradients.

EXPERIMENTS
Logistic regression on MNIST: Adam achieves lower training cost per epoch than
SGD with Nesterov momentum and AdaGrad. Multi-layer perceptron and CNN
experiments show similar faster convergence in training loss.
